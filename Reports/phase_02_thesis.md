# Phase 2 Thesis: Audio LSTM Model Training

## Strategic Intent
With face emotion classification proven feasible (albeit poorly) in Phase 1, Phase 2 asked: "Can we build a speech emotion classifier from the RAVDESS dataset?" The hypothesis was that Mel-Frequency Cepstral Coefficients (MFCCs) extracted from audio recordings would carry enough tonal information for an LSTM to learn emotional patterns. The success criterion was a trained `.h5` model file that could be loaded and invoked programmatically.

## Scope & Boundaries
Phase 2 consisted of a single script (`v4.py`) that performed the complete training pipeline: walk the `archive_6/` directory of RAVDESS `.wav` files, parse the RAVDESS filename convention to extract emotion codes, extract 40-band MFCCs padded/truncated to 174 frames, train a single-layer LSTM (128 units) with Dropout for 40 epochs, and save the result as `speech_emotion_model_7.h5`. The emotion mapping merged RAVDESS codes `01` (neutral) and `02` (calm) into a single "neutral" class, producing 7 output classes. No data augmentation was performed. No silence trimming was applied. No feature normalization was applied. The labels used `pd.get_dummies` for one-hot encoding (categorical crossentropy), unlike the later integer-label approach (sparse categorical crossentropy).

## Failure Analysis
Phase 2 "succeeded" in producing a loadable model file but failed in three critical ways that would haunt later phases: (1) No silence trimming meant 1-2 seconds of dead air at recording boundaries were included in the MFCC extraction, biasing the model toward "neutral" for any input with quiet segments. (2) No feature normalization meant the model was sensitive to absolute volume levels — louder recordings were systematically misclassified as "angry" (the "False Angry" bias). (3) No augmentation meant the model overfit to the pristine RAVDESS studio conditions and failed on real-world mobile phone audio. These three issues were not resolved until the Kaggle retraining (documented in `Model notebooks.txt`) which added `librosa.effects.trim(top_db=30)`, white noise injection, pitch shifting, and `compute_class_weight('balanced')`, producing `audio_best_model.keras` with **94.10% validation accuracy**. The Z-score normalization was added even later, in Phase 8's `main_audio.py`.
